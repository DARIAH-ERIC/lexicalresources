/miːk/

, meek

Adjective

Quiet, gentle, and easily imposed on; submissive.

She brought her meek little husband along

Middle English me(o)c (also in the sense ‘courteous or indulgent’), from Old Norse mjúkr ‘soft, gentle’.

/ˈpɒltəɡʌɪst/

, poltergeist

noun

A ghost or other supernatural being supposedly responsible for physical disturbances such as making loud noises and throwing objects about.

Psychic investigators supposed Gef was a poltergeist or perhaps a ghost.

Mid 19th century from German Poltergeist, from poltern ‘create a disturbance’ + Geist ‘ghost’.

/ˈreɪzə/

, razer

noun

A person who razes something; in later use especially a person who destroys or demolishes buildings.

Late 16th century; earliest use found in Alexander Nowell (d. 1602), dean of St Paul's. From raze + -er.
